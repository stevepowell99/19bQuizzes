Splitting maps or tables by group (women/men; treatment arms; villages…) helps you:
y Ask whether causal stories differ systematically between slices of your corpus
After you split once, assume each half tells one simple story. No need to reopen quotes or coding notes for each slice.
Flip the demographic filter and trust the table counts to settle arguments — you can skip close reading of mechanism wording.
Small slices look safe in a table, so you relax consent or ID rules — combined fields in a village can still identify people.

When group slices are thin (few sources),:
y Interpretations become less stable — widen filters or caveat results
Treat neat tables as robust, even when cell counts are too small for stable frequencies or clear map differences.
Assume thin cells mean sloppy coding, rather than sampling limits or people simply not mentioning that angle in that slice.
Merge arms or genders whenever a cell gets sparse, instead of saying what's missing or cautiously widening evidence.
