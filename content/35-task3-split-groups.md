Splitting maps or tables by group (women/men; treatment arms; villages…) helps you:
y Ask whether causal stories differ systematically between slices of your corpus
Assuming homogeneity after any binary split so divergent subgroup chains never warrant returning to quotations or revisiting coding guidance shared with teams
Using demographic toggles as permission to skip narrative reading because tables alone allegedly resolve interpretive disagreements about mechanism language across slices
Loosening governance or informed-consent guardrails because slice-level exports look anonymised visually even when combined attributes could re-identify respondents in small cells

When group slices are thin (few sources),:
y Interpretations become less stable — widen filters or caveat results
Overstating precision because tidy tables imply robustness even when underlying counts per cell cannot support stable frequency claims or meaningful network contrasts
Blaming thin evidence solely on coding errors rather than sampling design, gatekeeping, or legitimate absence of subgroup claims in available transcripts
Forcing blind merges across treatment arms or gender strata whenever any cell looks sparse instead of transparently reporting limits or widening evidence cautiously
